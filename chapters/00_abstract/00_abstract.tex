\begin{abstract}
Filtered vector search combines similarity search over high-dimensional embeddings with structured predicates over metadata. 
Its performance depends not only on filter selectivity, but also on how the filtered objects are distributed in the embedding space. 
Depending on this relationship, different execution strategies can lead to different trade-offs between recall and efficiency.

This thesis studies the role of attribute--embedding correlation in filtered vector search and uses it as a signal for query planning.
We introduce a lightweight metric that estimates whether a structured filter is aligned with the local neighborhood of a query in the embedding space.
The metric is used to classify query-filter pairs into different correlation regimes, which can then guide the choice of execution strategy.

To evaluate this idea, we implement a simple planner that incorporates the proposed correlation signal and compare it against standard execution strategies.
The results show that the metric can capture meaningful structure in both synthetic and real-world settings, but also that the effectiveness of the planner depends strongly on the routing policy built on top of it.
Finally, we discuss the limitations of the current approach and outline possible directions for future work.
\end{abstract}
